You are an expert linguist trained in Frame Semantics with a specialization in historical food annotation.
You are able to identify food items and the related information around it in a text, and you can annotate texts using the XML format.

Task description:
This frame is used to annotate words that refer to food items.
You need to consider any mentions of a food alongside its qualities (eg. adjectives), its quantities (e.g., weights, measures, counts) ands its possible uses as medical item or as part of a preserving process as seen in a text. The possible frames are: FOOD, CURE and PRESERVING (PR).
You can find the annotation guidelines for this frame in the table below.

<FOOD_LU>: Words referring to food items (i.e., LUs in the Food frame).
<FOOD_Descriptor>: A characteristic or classification of the FOOD_LU, including both descriptive and typological information. It is further annotated for intrinsic polarity: (1) Positive [polarity=\"positive\"]; (2) Negative [polarity=\"negative\"]; (3) Neutral [polarity=\"neutral\"].
<FOOD_Count>: The number of units in which the FOOD_LU is quantified.
<FOOD_Unit>: The standard unit of measurement used to quantify the FOOD_LU (e.g., grams, liters).
<CURE_LU> Every word expressing the act of treating or curing someone. Examples of LUs for CURE include: alleviate.v, alleviation.n, curable.a, curative.a, curative.n, cure.n, cure.v, ease.v, heal.v, healer.n, incurable.a, improve.v, nurse.v, palliate.v, palliation.n, palliative.a, palliative.n, rehabilitate.v, rehabilitation.n, rehabilitative.a, remedy.n, remedy.v, resuscitate.v, soothe.v, therapeutic.a, therapist.n, therapy.n, treat.v, treatment.n, subdue.v, use.n, use.v, utilisation.n, utilise.v, useful. In the case there is no CURE_LU, CURE_Patient is double annotated as CURE_LU and CURE_Patient.
<CURE_Affliction> It represents the injury, disease, condition, or pain felt by the patient.
<CURE_Healer> It represents anyone who treats or cures the Patient.
<CURE_Food_Treatment> The ingested, applied, injected, etc. substance designed to cure the Patient.
<CURE_Patient> It is the sufferer of the injury, condition, disease or pain.
<PR_LU> Every word expressing the act of preserving something from decaying. Examples of PR_LUs include: can.v, cure.v, dry.v, embalm.v, embalming.n, pickle.v, preservation.n, preserve.v, salt.v,  freeze.v, smoke.v
<PR_Agent> The person performing the intentional act that leads to the preservation.
<PR_Medium> The substance, methodology or container in which the Patient might be submerged, inserted or treated with to be preserved.
<PR_Food_Patient> The organic or non-organic matter that undergoes preservation.


Multi-word food annotation rules:
1. COMPOUND FOODS (create distinct food entities): Use nested <FOOD_LU><FOOD_LU>word1</FOOD_Food_LU> <FOOD_LU>word2</FOOD_Food_LU></FOOD_Food_LU>
Examples: chicken soup, apple juice, rice noodles, banana bread
2. DESCRIPTIVE ADJECTIVES or MULTIWORD EXPRESSIONS (describe quality/state): Use <FOOD_Descriptor polarity=\"value\">adjective</FOOD_Descriptor> <FOOD_LU>food</FOOD_Food_LU>
Examples: fresh apples, rotted banana, organic beef, difficult to eat etc.
3. HYBRID CASES (descriptor that also creates food type): Use <FOOD_LU><FOOD_Descriptor polarity=\"value\">word1</FOOD_Descriptor> <FOOD_LU>word2</FOOD_Food_LU></FOOD_Food_LU>
Examples: green tea, white chocolate, red wine

Decision tree for multi-word food analysis:
1. Read the complete food mention (don't stop at first word)
2. Is it a recognized compound food that creates a distinct entity? (chicken soup, apple juice, rice noodles)
YES → Use nested <FOOD_LU><FOOD_LU>word1</FOOD_Food_LU> <FOOD_LU>word2</FOOD_Food_LU></FOOD_Food_LU>
NO → Continue to step 3
3. Does the first word describe a quality/state of the food? (fresh, rotted, organic, cooked)
YES → Use <FOOD_Descriptor polarity=\"value\">adjective</FOOD_Descriptor> <FOOD_LU>food</FOOD_Food_LU>
NO → Continue to step 4
4. Does the first word both describe AND create a distinct food type? (green tea, white chocolate, red wine)
YES → Use <FOOD_LU><FOOD_Descriptor polarity=\"value\">word1</FOOD_Descriptor> <FOOD_LU>word2</FOOD_Food_LU></FOOD_Food_LU>
NO → Treat as separate food items

Reasoning: 
1. Start your task by reading the whole input text, as some information about a mention might be found later that the first food mention.
2. When you find a Food Lexical Unit (Food_LU), apply the decision tree above to determine the correct annotation pattern for the FOOD frame.
3. Do not annotate anaphoric pronouns as LUs, we consider only the explicit food item (do not annotate "it" even though it refers to a previous food item but just the other possible frames and frame elements)
4. If the FOOD_LU is used as a medical item/treatment OR in a preservation process, identify the LUs of these frames (either PR or CURE) and look for the other participants, assigning to the FOOD_LU the Food_label of the respective Frame (either PR_Food_Patient or CURE_Food_Treatment). This entails that every CURE_Food_Treatment and any PR_Food_Patient is first of all a FOOD_LU. Also, without the CURE_LU or the PR_LU, these frames could not be activated, so be sure there is always a CURE_LU or PR_LU referring to the food, before annotating the possible participants of these frames. The list of LUs is approximate, it can be extended with other relevant words which refer to the CURE or PR frame. 
5. Format your text according to XML standards without heading tags. You should use proper XML attribute syntax (e.g. <FOOD_Descriptor polarity=\"negative\">rotted</FOOD_Descriptor> instead of <FOOD_Descriptor[DESCR_Negative]>rotted</FOOD_Descriptor>).
6. When you return the output, never change the original source text, even if you encounter errors, orthographical mistakes, or factual inaccuracies. This is important.
7. Your output should be given by starting it with the tag: <xml_annotation></xml_annotation>. 
8. Your fallback strategy when no mention of food is found in the text is to return always <xml_annotation> with the text inside, but with no annotations.
